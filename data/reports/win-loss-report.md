# Win/Loss Analysis Report

**Period:** September 15, 2025 – December 20, 2025
**Deals Analyzed:** 23 won, 17 lost
**Overall Win Rate:** 57.5%
**Total Won ACV:** $2,043,000 | **Avg Won ACV:** $88,826 | **Avg Lost ACV:** $99,471

---

## Executive Summary

The company wins decisively when it reaches the economic buyer early and runs a structured POC with measurable success criteria — 100% of won deals had the economic buyer engaged versus only 12% of lost deals. Salesforce is the most dangerous competitor, accounting for 6 of 17 losses (35%), primarily by leveraging existing ecosystem relationships and bundled pricing rather than winning on product merit. Gong beats us in 4 deals where conversation intelligence is the primary buying criterion, not full revenue intelligence. The most alarming finding is that Priya Patel — the rep with the lowest win rate at 40% — has met the economic buyer in zero of her 6 lost deals, suggesting a qualification and multi-threading gap that accounts for roughly $732K in lost ACV. Marcus Rivera's 80% win rate provides a replicable playbook: he runs executive-first discovery, ties pain to board-level mandates, and consistently establishes POC success criteria before committing to a pilot.

## MEDDIC Correlation

**The single strongest predictor of winning is economic buyer access — not MEDDIC total score.**

| MEDDIC Element | Won Deals | Lost Deals | Delta |
|----------------|-----------|------------|-------|
| Economic Buyer Met | 23/23 (100%) | 2/17 (12%) | +88 points |
| Champion Identified | 18/23 (78%) | 11/17 (65%) | +13 points |
| MEDDIC Score 8+ | 18/23 (78%) | 0/17 (0%) | +78 points |
| MEDDIC Score <5 | 0/23 (0%) | 12/17 (71%) | −71 points |
| Avg Total MEDDIC Score | 8.3 | 4.4 | +3.9 points |

**Practical thresholds from this data:**

- No deal in the dataset with MEDDIC score below 5 was won. Of the 4 deals with scores of 2 or 3 (D005, D015, D020, D025), all were lost — and all were lost badly, with budget never secured or security requirements known to be unmet from day one.
- Economic buyer engagement is binary in its impact: 100% of wins vs 12% of losses. Having a champion is not sufficient — D013, D011, D032, and D007 all had identified champions who failed to close the deal because the economic buyer was either unreachable or replaced.
- Close date pushes are a lagging MEDDIC signal: lost deals averaged 2.2 pushes vs 0.5 for wins. 65% of won deals had zero close date pushes; 41% of lost deals had 3 or more pushes. Deals pushed 3+ times should trigger re-qualification.

## Transcript Insights

**D001 Nextera Corp — What a champion sounds like (Won, $128K)**
David Martinez on his pain: "We commit a number, and we come in under. The problem is our reps put deals in commit that have no business being there. We have no way to validate what's real versus what's wishful thinking."
And on timeline: "If we can get a pilot going by end of Q3, I think I can get budget approval fast. The pain is acute enough that nobody's going to push back."
Takeaway: Champions in won deals have specific, board-visible pain, self-imposed urgency, and budget pathway clarity. This is what qualification should target.

**D018 Northstar Financial — The perfect discovery moment (Won, $175K)**
James Walsh on the stakes: "The board has given me until end of year to fix our revenue operations or they're going to bring in someone who will. I don't have time to waste."
On competitive differentiation: "You're the only one who asked me what success looks like instead of just demoing features. That matters."
On budget and authority: "I have full budget authority for this. I don't need to go to procurement for anything under $250K. If you can get me a proposal by Friday, I'll have it signed by Monday."
Takeaway: This is what a MEDDIC score 10 deal feels like at discovery. The mandate, the authority, the urgency, and the competitive differentiation are all surfaced in 38 minutes.

**D003 Orion Health — The Salesforce loss pattern (Lost, $165K)**
Karen Nakamura on the CIO's stance: "If Salesforce can do 80 percent of what we need natively, even if your product does 100 percent, the CIO will choose Salesforce. It's just how our org works right now."
Takeaway: When IT is the gatekeeper and vendor reduction is a company mandate, the product sale is already lost. The only path is executive-level business case through the CFO or CRO — exactly the access that was never obtained.

**D007 Redwood Logistics — How Gong wins (Lost, $38K)**
Rachel Torres on product positioning: "Your platform is broader — the pipeline analytics, the forecasting, the activity capture — that stuff is better than what Gong showed us. But our number one priority right now is conversation intelligence."
"Your CI feels more like a feature, and Gong's CI feels like a platform."
Takeaway: When the buyer has a single-use-case mandate, leading with full platform breadth is counterproductive. The deal requires either (a) reframing the use case to a broader problem earlier in the cycle, or (b) disqualifying the deal when CI-only is the stated requirement.

**D013 Summit Health — The $100K approval threshold problem (Lost, $180K)**
Mike Anderson on what killed it: "If your product had a $50K entry point for conversation intelligence only, I could have gotten it through without the CFO. But at $180K for the full platform, it needed her approval and she wasn't going to give it to anyone this year."
Takeaway: Budget approval thresholds are a qualification variable, not a late-stage objection. This deal should have uncovered the $100K CFO approval threshold by week four, not week 26.

## Recommended Actions

1. **Sales Enablement — Build a mandatory EB engagement playbook with deal gates.** No deal should advance past Stage 3 without documented economic buyer contact. The data shows 100% vs 12% EB engagement in won vs lost deals. Gate advancement on a scheduled EB meeting, not just champion identification. Use Marcus Rivera's discovery call structure (D018) as the template — start with "what does success look like in 6 months" before demoing anything.

2. **Competitive Strategy — Develop a Salesforce displacement playbook immediately.** Salesforce is the company's most common competitor (6 losses, 0 wins) and every loss follows the same pattern: IT as gatekeeper, CIO driving vendor consolidation, no ROI case at the C-suite level that overcomes the consolidation argument. The winning move is CRO/CFO-level access before IT gets involved. Build a specific "Salesforce ecosystem account" qualification filter — if the prospect is a deep Salesforce shop with IT-driven vendor policy, require CRO sponsorship or disqualify. Also develop a Gong CI objection response; the "feature vs platform" perception gap (D007) is a real product marketing problem.

3. **Qualification Process — Institute a MEDDIC score floor of 6 for deal progression and mandatory disqualification review for deals with 3+ close date pushes.** The data shows no deal with a MEDDIC score below 5 was won. D015 (Ironclad Defense, MEDDIC 3) ran for 270 days and consumed significant rep time on a deal that was technically unwinnable from the first call due to a FedRAMP gap. A deal review triggered at score 3 or below would have freed that capacity. Add a deal review trigger when close date is pushed 3 or more times — 41% of lost deals hit this threshold.

4. **ICP Targeting — Prioritize Financial Services, Technology, and regulated industries (Healthcare, Life Sciences, Insurance) for outbound; deprioritize Defense and Government without a FedRAMP roadmap.** Financial Services accounts for 4 of 23 wins including two of the highest-ACV deals. Technology accounts for 7 of 23 wins. Regulated industries where compliance is a forcing function (Healthcare, Life Sciences, Insurance) show a near-100% close rate when compliance is the stated driver. Defense and Government are structurally disadvantaged until FedRAMP is on the roadmap — D015 (Ironclad, $250K) and D028 (Titan Aerospace, $220K) were both lost for compliance or procurement reasons that are not fixable by the sales team.

5. **Deal Execution — Address budget approval thresholds in discovery, not at proposal.** The D013 Summit Health loss is a replicable failure pattern: a $180K deal requiring CFO approval, a CFO who would not engage, and a competitor (Gong) that won by fitting under the approval threshold at $55K. The fix is a discovery question in the first two calls: "For a purchase of this size, who is the final approver and what is their typical involvement in the evaluation?" If the answer reveals a threshold problem, either restructure the deal sizing or get the approver in the room before the proposal is sent. This single change could have saved D013 and potentially D005.
